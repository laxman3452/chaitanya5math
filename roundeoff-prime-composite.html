Number Sense: Rounding, Prime & Composite Numbers

Grade: Class 5
Subject: Mathematics
Duration: 80 Minutes

Phase 1: Anticipatory Set & Introduction to Rounding

Real-World Scenario:

“Imagine a vehicle runs out of fuel at point C, which is 1,360 meters away from Station A (at 1,300m) and 40 meters away from Station B (at 1,400m). Which station should it go to?”

Answer: It should go to Station B (1,400m) because it is much closer!

Teaching Note: Explain to students that finding the "closest cleaner number" is what we call Rounding Off in mathematics.

Number Line Demonstration

Look at the board context: If we have a number line from 1,420 to 1,430 and we mark 1,424...

Answer: It sits closer to 1,420!

Phase 2: Core Instruction – Rounding Rules

The Place Value Rules:

  • Look at the digit directly to the right of your target place value.
  • If it is 5 or more → Add 1 to the target digit and change all following digits to 0.
  • If it is less than 5 → Leave the target digit the same and change all following digits to 0.

Guided Example 1: Nearest Hundred

Round 4,630 to the nearest hundred.

Look at the tens digit: 3 (less than 5).

Answer: 4,600

Guided Example 2: Nearest Thousand

Round 32,536 to the nearest thousand.

Look at the hundreds digit: 5 (5 or more).

Answer: 33,000

The Tie-Breaker Rules

Round 46,500 to the nearest thousand. What happens when it's exactly in the middle?

Because 500 is exactly in the middle, standard mathematical convention always rounds UP.

Answer: 47,000

Phase 3: Guided Practice – Rounding

1. Fill in the Gaps

The thousand below 6,943 is _____ and the thousand above is _____.

The thousand below is 6,000 and the thousand above is 7,000.

2. True or False Challenge

“2,500 becomes 2,000 when rounded to the nearest thousand.”

Answer: FALSE. It becomes 3,000 because 5 rounds up!

3. Quick Notebook Practice

Round 1,250 to the nearest hundred.

Answer: 1,300

Phase 4: Core Instruction & Activity – Prime vs. Composite

Prime Numbers

Numbers that have exactly two factors: 1 and the number itself.

Composite Numbers

Numbers that have more than two factors. They can be divided evenly by other numbers.

Interactive Activity: Sieve of Eratosthenes (1 to 50)

Interactive Demo Instruction: Click once to Circle (Prime), click twice to Cross Out (Composite/Eliminated), click again to Reset.

Answer: Number 1 only has ONE factor (itself). Therefore, it is neither prime nor composite!

Phase 5: Deep Dive & Conceptual Checks

Q1: Is 19 prime or composite? Why?

Prime. It only has two factors: 1 and 19.

Q2: Is 9 prime or composite? Why?

Composite. It has three factors: 1, 3, and 9.

Q3: What is the only even prime number?

2 (All other even numbers can be divided evenly by 2, making them composite).

Advanced Application Exploration

Let's find the greatest prime number from 1 to 20: 19

  • What happens if we add 1 to it? (19 + 1 = 20) → Composite
  • What happens if we subtract 2 from it? (19 - 2 = 17) → Prime
